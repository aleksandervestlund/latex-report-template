\section{Math Examples}
This section demonstrates various mathematical features supported by the template. Every package listed under the \enquote{Mathematics} category in \mintinline{latex}{packages.sty} is represented here with a usage example. Some of these examples are purely illustrative and are not necessarily mathematically meaningful.

The \gls{gcd} and \gls{lcm} are fundamental concepts in number theory. The \gls{lcm} of two numbers is the smallest number that is a multiple of both.
\begin{equation*}
    \gcd\del{a, b} = \mathit{\glsxtrshort{gcd}}\del{a, b}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    \lcm\del{a, b} = \mathit{\glsxtrshort{lcm}}\del{a, b}
\end{equation*}

We begin with examples of custom\-/defined relation symbols:
\begin{equation*}
    x \defeq y + z,
    \quad a \whateq b,
    \quad p \hateq q,
    \quad f \coloneqq g,
    \quad M \meq N,
    \quad U \qmeq V
\end{equation*}

Superscript shorthands for inverse and transpose:
\begin{equation*}
    A\inv,
    \quad A\transpose,
    \quad {A\inv}\transpose
\end{equation*}

The \mintinline{latex}{defn} command is used to introduce a \defn{defined term} in bold italic.
The \mintinline{latex}{amortized} command denotes amortized cost: $\amortized{T}\del{n}$.

The number of permutations of $r$ items from a set of $n$ elements is given by:
\begin{equation*}
    \perm{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{\del{n - r}!}
\end{equation*}

The number of combinations (binomial coefficient) is:
\begin{equation*}
    \choose{n}{r} = \comb{n}{r} = \frac{n!}{r!\del{n - r}!}
\end{equation*}

We can also use floor and ceiling functions:
\begin{equation*}
    \ceil*{1.2} = 2, \qquad \floor*{2.2} = 2
\end{equation*}

The \mintinline{latex}{dell}, \mintinline{latex}{dellr}, and \mintinline{latex}{delr} commands add tight spacing around \mintinline{latex}{del} delimiters:
\begin{equation*}
    f\del{x},
    \quad f\del{x},
    \quad f\del{x}
\end{equation*}

The \mintinline{latex}{narrowarray} and \mintinline{latex}{normalarray} commands adjust column spacing in arrays:
\begin{equation*}
    \narrowarray{0.5}
    \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}
    \normalarray
    \quad \text{vs} \quad
    \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation*}

\subsection{Expected Value and Variance}

Expected value and variance:
\begin{equation*}
    \expect{X} = \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} x \cdot \prob{X = x}
\end{equation*}
\begin{equation*}
    \Var\del{X} = \sigmasq\del{X} = \expect{\dell{X - \expect{X}}^2} = \expectsq{X} - \expect{X}^2
\end{equation*}

\subsection{Theorem Environments}

The template declares a wide range of theorem-like environments using \mintinline{latex}{thmtools} and \mintinline{latex}{amsthm}. All \--- except remarks (arbitrarily) \--- are numbered within sections.

\begin{theorem}[Extreme Value Theorem]
    Let $f$ be a continuous function on a closed interval $[a,b]$. Then $f$ attains both its maximum and minimum on $[a,b]$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    By the Extreme Value Theorem, a continuous function on a compact set attains its bounds.
\end{proof}
\begin{proofidea}
    Compactness of $[a,b]$ under continuity forces boundedness and attainment.
\end{proofidea}
\begin{proofsketch}
    Cover $[a,b]$ by open sets, extract a finite subcover, and simply apply continuity.
\end{proofsketch}
